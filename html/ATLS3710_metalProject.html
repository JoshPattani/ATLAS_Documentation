For this project I wanted to make a wood burning rocket stove. The metal used was sourced from the scrap pile behind my employer's fabrication shop.


The first step was to cut the metal to size. I used a stationary metal cutting bandsaw to cut the larger tube steel. For the angle and flat stock I used the 32 ton ironworker break press to shear cut the metal to size.




After cutting all the pieces, I welded the square tube pieces together, as well as the grill insert. I used a Miller 210 Millermatic solid core metal inert gas-shielded welding machine, with 100% CO2 gas to achieve the weldments.

My phone died in the midst of fitting and welding these pieces. In result, I was unable to capture this process.

Mild-Steel Mallard disapproves of my irresponsibility to document.




For the air intake I made a multi-functional slide from 4"x4"x0.125" flat stock that will also allow me to empty the ashes more easily from the bottom of the stove.



The stove was slightly front heavy, and I feared it could tip when in use. As a solution I attached a front leg on with a repurposed piece of 3"x3"x0.187" angle bracket I had sitting around at home.



Half inch holes were drilled in the bottom of the stove to bolt on the front leg I fabricated. Starting with a 3/32" drill bit I stepped through a series of 5 drill bits up to 1/2".



The final step will be to paint the stove with high temperature paint. But, I didn't get the chance to test out the stove before snow arrived and wet up all the sticks. Upon successfully testing of the air choke under high heat, I will coat the stove to prevent rusting.

My arms will certainly be pumped by the end of my walk to class, but here's the final product!
